My Story: The Wake-Up Call

A Personal Narrative

Before coming to Wake Forest, I had never lived immersed in nature. Growing up in a city, it was here that I was first genuinely surrounded by towering trees, ever-changing blue skies, sunlight filtering through the leaves, and the cheerful, unscared squirrels.

A Serene Dream

It all formed a serene, harmonious dream, leading me to believe that this was how our world was supposed to be—beautiful and balanced. The campus became my first true long-time encounter with the natural world, a place where I could witness the quiet rhythms of life unfolding around me.

"However, this tranquility became the prelude to my awakening. As I began to truly understand our planet, this beautiful illusion was completely shattered."

The Shattering Truth

I learned a shocking truth: we are currently living through the sixth mass extinction, and this time, the driving force is not an asteroid or volcanoes, but us—humanity.

This realization filled me with a deep and profound anger. The beautiful campus I saw was a facade masking global deforestation and habitat fragmentation. The lively squirrels around me hinted at the countless species being silenced forever due to habitat loss, pollution, and climate chaos.

I understood that the harmony I enjoyed was a dangerous illusion, masking the systematic collapse occurring in other corners of the Earth.

Forging Determination

It is this anger that forged my unwavering determination. It made it impossible for me to remain comfortable in this beautiful "bubble." It made it clear that we must do something together to change this trajectory.

This is my "Why."

02

My Philosophical Compass

Why I Act

My commitment to protecting our planet is anchored in two complementary philosophical frameworks. One defines the value of what I seek to protect, and the other defines the person I strive to become in that effort.

Ecocentrism

Recognizing Intrinsic Value

Ecocentrism asserts that nature has inherent worth, completely independent of its utility to humans. This philosophy stands in direct opposition to the worldview that treats the planet merely as a resource for human consumption.

My Connection:

This idea gave a name to the source of my anger. Learning about the sixth mass extinction wasn't just learning about a "problem"; it was learning about the systematic destruction of something that has a right to exist for its own sake.

The squirrels on campus, the untouched wetlands, the complex web of life in the oceans—their value isn't contingent on how they serve us. My mission, therefore, is not just to preserve a "pleasant environment for humans," but to defend a living world that possesses its own inherent dignity.

Virtue Ethics

Cultivating an Ecological Character

Virtue ethics shifts the focus from rules or consequences to the character of the moral agent. It asks not "What should I do?" but "Who should I be?"

My Connection:

This framework validates the emotional core of my "Why." My love for nature is not a sentimental sidebar; it is the virtue of benevolence extended to the more-than-human world. My anger at its destruction is not an irrational emotion; it is the virtue of justice reacting to a profound wrong. My determination to act is the virtue of courage in the face of a daunting challenge.

Protecting the planet, for me, is the essential practice of becoming a more virtuous, integrated, and responsible human being.

Synthesis: A Unified Foundation

Together, these philosophies provide a powerful, dual foundation for my climate action. Ecocentrism tells me what is worthy of protection, and virtue ethics tells me who I must become to be an effective protector.

My "Why" is, therefore, both a commitment to the intrinsic value of the living world and a commitment to my own moral growth within it.

03

My Commitment: From Awareness to Practice

Translating Philosophy into Action

Conviction without action is incomplete. My philosophical framework demands tangible expression. These three commitments translate my understanding of ecocentrism and virtue ethics into practices that shape my daily life and my contribution to the world.

01

Defend Intrinsic Value

If nature possesses inherent worth independent of human use, then my actions must reflect that belief. This means actively defending the right of species and ecosystems to exist—not because they benefit me, but because their existence is valuable in itself.

  • Support conservation efforts that prioritize biodiversity over human recreation
  • Advocate for policies that grant legal standing to ecosystems
  • Challenge language that frames nature solely as "natural resources"
  • Make choices that minimize my footprint on wild habitats
02

Practice Ecological Virtue

Virtue ethics teaches that character is built through practice. I commit to cultivating the virtues of benevolence, justice, and courage toward the more-than-human world—not as a one-time decision, but as a daily discipline that shapes who I am becoming.

  • Practice benevolence through daily acts of care for local nature
  • Exercise justice by speaking out against ecological destruction
  • Develop courage by taking uncomfortable stands for the voiceless
  • Cultivate humility by recognizing my place within, not above, nature
03

Bridge the Illusion

My awakening began when the "beautiful illusion" was shattered. Most people still live within that bubble, unaware of the sixth mass extinction unfolding beyond their view. My commitment is to help others see—not through guilt or fear, but through connection and truth.

  • Share my story and invite others to reflect on theirs
  • Create conversations that make the invisible crisis visible
  • Connect the beauty we enjoy to the destruction we ignore
  • Build community around shared love for the living world
04

Living Awake

I began in an illusion—the serene dream of Wake Forest's towering trees, blue skies, and cheerful squirrels. I believed this beauty was simply how the world was. Then the illusion shattered, and I saw the truth: we are living through the sixth mass extinction, and we are its cause.

That awakening could have led to despair. Instead, it led me to philosophy—to ecocentrism, which taught me that the natural world possesses inherent worth beyond any human measure, and to virtue ethics, which showed me that my love, my anger, and my determination are not obstacles to moral clarity but its very foundation.

Now, I understand my path. Ecocentrism tells me what is worthy of protection: a living world with its own right to exist. Virtue ethics tells me who I must become: a person whose character is forged through the daily practice of ecological benevolence, justice, and courage. My commitments—defending intrinsic value, practicing ecological virtue, and bridging the illusion for others—are not burdens. They are the expression of a fully awake life.

"To live awake is not to carry the weight of the world, but to carry its love—and to act from that love with clear eyes and an unwavering heart."

The crisis is real. The loss is immense. But I refuse to look away, and I refuse to be paralyzed. I have seen the beauty, I have faced the truth, and I have found my "Why." This is not the end of a project—it is the beginning of a commitment. A commitment to the intrinsic value of the living world, and to my own moral growth within it.

— With clear eyes and determined hope, for the world that deserves our protection.
